Design for AI

Designing the interface an AI lives inside

Copilot panels, agent dashboards, operator consoles. The hard part was never the model, it's the surface around it, and how to make a probabilistic system feel trustworthy without pretending it's certain.

June 2026·7 min read

Every team I've worked with wanted to talk about the model first. The interface, they figured, was the easy part: drop a text box on the page and let the magic happen. It never works out that way.

The model is a capability. The interface is the product. The gap between those two sentences is where most AI features quietly fail, and it's where almost all of the design work actually lives.

01 The model isn't the product

When I designed internal tooling at Meta, the underlying model could do far more than anyone used it for. Capability was never the bottleneck. Trust was. People won't hand a real decision to a system they can't read, predict, or correct.

So the first questions were never about prompts or parameters. They were about surface: how much to reveal, when to interrupt, what to do when the model is confidently, fluently wrong. Those are interface decisions, and they decide whether anyone ever uses the thing twice.

A model that's right 90% of the time is a demo. An interface that handles the other 10% gracefully is a product.

02 Show the work, not the magic

The instinct with AI is to hide the machinery and present a clean answer. That reads beautifully in a demo and erodes trust in production. The interfaces that actually got adopted did the opposite, they showed their work:

  • Surface the sources, so a person can sanity-check the claim in two seconds.
  • Expose confidence honestly, including when it's low, instead of one flat tone.
  • Make every AI action reversible, so trying it costs nothing.

None of that is a model improvement. It's all interface, and it's what moved a tool from "neat" to "I use this every day."

03 Design for being wrong

Deterministic software has bugs. Probabilistic software has a confidence distribution, which means being wrong isn't an edge case, it's a Tuesday. The whole experience has to assume the model will miss and stay calm when it does.

Practically, that meant designing the failure states first, not last: the empty result, the low-confidence answer, the "I'm not sure, here's what I'd check." When those states feel considered, the occasional wrong answer reads as honesty rather than betrayal.

04 What I'd tell my past self

Start from the surface, not the model. Sketch the moment a person reads the output and decides whether to trust it, because that moment is the product. The model will keep getting better on its own. The interface only gets better if a designer fights for it.

That's the throughline across everything I've shipped in this space: the AI is the easy half. The honest, legible, forgiving surface around it is the work.
